00 ‚Äî The State of GEO

Lots of theories,
little data.

Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) has a speculation problem. The field is still new, the research is still limited, and the "best practices" being floated around to help you show up in AI search seem to change every five minutes. Every digital marketing agency has an answer for GEO. Almost none of them have evidence.

We decided to find some. Two simple questions guided our experiment: When ChatGPT recommends brands, which ones does it pick? And can we figure out why?

01 ‚Äî The Setup

What happens when 10 people ask ChatGPT the same question?

We ran the same experiment across two separate industries: fast-food dining and digital marketing agencies. (Okay yes, that second category was a bit self-serving‚Äîwe're a marketing agency after all. We wanted to know if ChatGPT was recommending our team, and if so, to who.)

For each industry, we build five distinct, detailed customer personas that included personal background, budget, decision context, and relationship to the category. Each persona asked ChatGPT the same 25 questions, divided among five intent types (see Appendices 6-7). To improve the sample, each persona posed each query three times. And we logged every single brand mention and reason cited.

03 ‚Äî Personalized Recommendations

When ChatGPT recommends you, why?

Even though we ran the same controlled experiment across both categories, the results looked very different. Here are the patterns that emerged.

Pattern #1 ‚Äî Routing
Different buyers get routed to
entirely different brands.

This was true in both categories, but the pattern was especially pronounced in the fragmented agency market. ChatGPT doesn't appear to work from a single list and filter down‚Äîinstead, it produces completely independent competitive universes depending on who's asking.

Pattern #2 ‚Äî Brand Ubiquity
Cross-persona presence is rare ‚Äî
and meaningful when it exists.

When a brand is recommended widely across personas, it reflects a significant competitive advantage. In the fast food category, many of the winning brands have established such a level of cultural ubiquity that ChatGPT reaches for them regardless of context. In the more specialized agency market, most brands struggled to span buyer types.

Persona Specificity ‚Äî How Many Buyer Types Does Each Brand Reach?
Major fast food brands tend to reach multiple buyer types ‚Äî 38% appear across 4 or 5 personas. Agency brands are far more specialized: 84% appear for one persona only. ChatGPT routes agency buyers to completely different competitive universes depending on who's asking the same question.

Pattern #3 ‚Äî Rationale
Even when the same brand appears for multiple buyers,
the reasoning changes.

An agency recommended to a startup founder gets framed around demand generation and pipeline velocity. Recommended to a franchise development VP, the same brand surfaces in the context of multi-location systems and local market coverage. In the fast food segment, the same chain gets recommended to one buyer for speed and app convenience, while it's recommended to another for nutritional transparency.


In other words, ChatGPT doesn't just recommend "the best" brand. It recommends the brand that best fits the buyer.

04 ‚Äî Case Study

Same company.
Different planet.

Christine and Derek both work at franchise companies. Same industry, same org chart. When you look at who the AI routes them to, they might as well be in different markets entirely.

Christine's problem is coordination at scale ‚Äî managing brand standards and multi-location consistency across hundreds of franchisees. 05 ‚Äî The Hypothesis Phase 1 Generated

We noticed a pattern.
So we tested it.

The per-persona routing in Phase 1 pointed at a mechanism. KickStart Dental gets routed to Ray because its entire web presence speaks dental practice growth language. Refine Labs gets routed to Jordan because its content speaks B2B SaaS demand generation. The content seemed to matter ‚Äî not just the brand's size or fame.

Our hypothesis: if you measure how closely a brand's website language overlaps with what ChatGPT says about that brand to a specific buyer, that overlap score should predict how often the AI recommends the brand to that buyer. Brands speaking their buyer's language clearly should get routed more. Brands with a mismatch should get routed less ‚Äî or get recommended despite their site, not because of it.

To test this, we crawled the top brands from Phase 1 and built what we call a content alignment score: a measure of how conceptually close a brand's website content is to the AI's responses for each buyer type, on a 0‚Äì100 scale. Think of it as measuring whether the brand and the AI are speaking the same language for a given buyer. A score of 60 means they're using very similar vocabulary and concepts. A score of 30 means they're in different conceptual worlds.

How we measured it

Both the brand website content and the AI's persona responses were converted to high-dimensional numerical vectors using the same embedding model (text-embedding-3-large). We then measured the cosine similarity between them ‚Äî essentially the angle between two vectors in a 3,072-dimensional space. A score near 1 (we scale to 100) means the language is pointing in the same direction. A score near 0 means they're pointing in different directions entirely. The result is a number between 0 and 100 that captures conceptual overlap without requiring an exact vocabulary match.

Did the hypothesis hold?

Yes ‚Äî in our QSR Phase 2 sample, brands with higher content alignment scores tended to receive more persona-specific recommendations. We measured this using Spearman's rho (œÅ), a standard statistical measure of correlation that runs from ‚àí1 (perfectly inverse) to +1 (perfectly aligned). A value of 0 means no relationship at all. Anything above 0.5 is generally considered a strong positive correlation in social science research.

One honest caveat: correlation doesn't prove causation. The relationship could reflect direct causation ‚Äî your content shapes the training signal. Or both could reflect the same signals absorbed from the broader web. The recommended action is the same either way. And as more AI tools retrieve live content at query time, the path from your site to the AI's answer is getting shorter.

Phase 2 ‚Äî Hypothesis Test
06 ‚Äî Punching Above Your Weight

Brand ubiquity matters.
Until content alignment matters more.

In our QSR sample, web authority ‚Äî the standard measure of how much of the web is talking about a brand ‚Äî does correlate with total AI mention volume. Bigger brands get mentioned more overall. That makes sense: the AI learned from the web, and brands with the deepest presence have the deepest training signal.

But web authority doesn't tell you which buyers the AI routes to you. Content alignment does. When we looked at both together, they turned out to be almost completely independent ‚Äî meaning a brand can have enormous web authority and poor content alignment, or limited web authority and strong alignment. They don't move together, so they can be addressed separately.

That independence is the opening for brands that don't have household-name authority. You can't close a domain authority gap in a quarter. You can close a content alignment gap. And for specialized markets, a sufficiently specific content strategy can outperform brands with dramatically more web presence ‚Äî for the buyer those brands aren't speaking to.
